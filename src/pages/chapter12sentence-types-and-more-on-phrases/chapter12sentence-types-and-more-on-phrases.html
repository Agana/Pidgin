Chapter 12 - Sentence Types and More on Phrases

Sentence Types & More on Phrases

Before considering how the various types of sentences work in Pidgin, it is useful to review two more types of phrases: adverbial and adjectival.

Adverbial Phrases

Adverbial Phrases Pidgin English does not often translate adverbial phrases like:

As long as As a matter of fact In/for the meantime Inasmuch as On second thought Rather than

Such phrases are used in their normal English form. Notice for example that in “As a matter of fact” the indefinite article “a” is preserved, even though it does not normally exist in Pidgin.

The same can be said of phrases that act as adjectives:

They are a very badly dressed couple

“Very badly dressed is an adverbial phrase, but it describes “couple” like an adjective. Pidgin would say:

Them be a very badly dressed * couple.

Again, notice that the article is preserved.

Sentence Types Based on Function

Sentences are also classified according to their grammatical complexity. Again there are four groups:

  • Simple sentences
  • Compound sentences
  • Complex sentences
  • Compound-Complex sentences

Simple Sentences

These are sentences that contain only one independent clause. An independent clause is one that conveys full meaning without the need of any other clauses. A good example of a simple sentence is:

  • I go school for the rain inside   (I went to school in the rain)

This sentence has only one independent clause. Simple sentences are not necessarily short. For example:

  • As a soldier with a duty to protect and an honour to preserve, you need to be fearless.
