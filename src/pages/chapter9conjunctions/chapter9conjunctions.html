Chapter 9 - Conjunctions

Conjunctions

Things are joined if they are simply attached to one another. They are conjoined if they are attached by an intervening entity that is shared among them. Conjunctions are such entities in grammar. Some of them are the so called fanboys: for, and, nor but, or, yet, so. But there are others not often thought of as conjunctions: even though, although, after, before, until, as long as.

As you can see, the full list would be almost endless, particularly in the second group. The first group is known as coordinating conjunctions.

Coordinating Conjunctionsg

These words join parts of a sentence that are of the same grammatical type or status. For example:

  • Tall or handsome    [adjective + adjective]

  • Akeela and the Bee     [noun + noun]

  • All my lands and some of yours       [phrase + phrase]

  • Lovely yet mean    [adjective + adjective]

  • Quickly but poorly    [adverb + adverb]

  • Go to school and study hard     [clause + clause]

Some examples:

Bonnie and Clide will never come back

I went to the mall and bought a bike

I have an injury yet I must persist

In Pidgin English when coordinating conjunctions join words and phrases, the construction stays the same, except the term “den/then” is used instead:

  • Tall or handsome

  • Akeela den the Bee

  • All my lands den some of your own

  • Lovely yet mean

  • Quickly but poorly

  • Bonnie den Clide never go return

However, when “and” joins verbal clauses, it is often –but not always - dropped:

She go come (den) worry you               (She will come and worry you)

I go visit you (den) cook give you        (I will visit you and cook for you)

The others are used as normal:

  • I get injury yet I for persist   (I have an injury yet I must persist)

  • Come house righdee or I go come find you    (Come home now or I will come find you)

  • I pay the bill so you fit relax     (I’ve paid the bill so you can relax)

A Word on Den (“And”)

The term den/then in regular English is used once before the last word in a list of similar grammatical units. In Pidgin English it is common to use it after each word in a list no matter how long. But it can also be used in the normal way.

  • John den Skinny den him bro go town (John, Skinny and his brother have gone to town)

  • Or much less commonly:

  • John, Skinny den him bro go town

Coordinating Correlative Conjunctions

These are coordinating conjunctions consisting of words that correlate with each other either positively or negatively. The commonest common coordinating correlatives are the positive “both… and” and “either… or” and the negative “neither… nor”. The other positive correlative “Not only… but also” has no real Pidgin equivalent.

In Pidgin English, “Either… or” and “neither… nor” are used the same way as they are in regular English:

  • Either your money or your fame go undo you

    (Either your money or your fame go undo you)

  • Neither friend nor foe fit dissuade me (Neither friend nor foe can dissuade me)

However “both… and” is not all that common, even though valid (in which case it would be “both… den”. The more common form is to use “den” with “all”. For example:

I can live in both poverty and wealth

becomes

I fit life for poverty den wealth all inside

Also:

  • My poppie den mommie all be Ghanaian (Both my father and mother are Ghanaian)

  • E fit play for the right den left wing all   (He can play on both right and left wings)

Like we’ve said, it is also valid to say:

  • Both my poppie den mommie be Ghanaian

or even

  • Both my poppie den mommie all the Ghanaians

If and If… Then

Use of the “if” and the “if… then” correlative of condition varies slightly in Pidgin. “If” sometimes takes the same form, as in:

  • If you beat am, I go chook you    (If you beat him, I will report you.)

But more often it will take the particle “a” at the end of the condition, sometimes dropping off the “if” entirely:

  • If you beat am a, I go chook you

or more commonly:

  • You beat am a, I go chook you

More on “Both”

As you know, the word “both” is also often used not as a proper conjunction but as a sort of adverb of addition, if you like. For example:

  • They are both here

In such cases Pidgin replaces “both” with “all”.

  • Them all dey here

Subordinating Conjunctions

In our introduction to conjunctions we saw a second group including phrases like “even though” and “as long as”, and words like “although”, “until” and “after”. These are called subordinating conjunctions because they tend to introduce subordinate clauses. For example:

  • I went to work even though it rained heavily today.

In this sentence, “Even though it rained heavily” cannot stand on its own, and is subordinate to “I went to work.” The phrase that makes it subordinate is the conjunction “even though”. This is why it’s called a subordinator, or subordinating conjunction.

There isn’t much to say about variants in Pidgin because the use of subordinators is generally the same. However, for the subordinating conjunction “even though”, Pidgin often reverts to a coordinated structure using “still”, or “but still”. So the above sentence would be:

  • E rain heavy today, but still I go work.

And less commonly:

  • I go work today even though e rain heavy.

When it comes to “even if”, Pidgin uses the enigmatic express “koraa a”, which is borrowed from Twi.

  • I will go there even if it is dangerous

becomes

  • E be dangerous koraa a, I go go there

A Note on “Koraa”

The general use of “Koraa” does not really fall under this topic, but it’s worth getting acquainted with the term now that we’ve used it. “Koraa” translates most times to the idiomatic adverbs of emphasis “at all”, “in any way”. It is also used sometimes for “completely”, especially when some form of denegration, degradation or destruction is intended. In general it is used to convey a sense of thoroughness or comprehensiveness. For example:

  • E no talk koraa    (He did not say anything at all)

  • A no dey mind koraa    (I don’t mind at all)

  • E no be possible koraa    (It is not possible in any way)

The kiddie no dey pet (or respect) koraa    (The child is not respectful at all)

And also:

  • I destroy am koraa   (I have destroyed it completely)

  • She yawa am koraa    (She completely embarrassed him)

Subordinating Correlative Conjunctions

There are also subordinating conjunctions that correlate with each other. Some of them are: “on the one hand… on the other (hand)” and “not only… but also”. Pidgin uses them as regular English does:

On the one hand I for save money; on the other hand I promise say I go buy am present.

(On the one hand I have to save money; on the other hand I promised to buy her a present.)

“Not only… but also” and “hardly… when” with their tweaks on verb tenses are rather too rich for Pidgin. They have no equivalents. Pidgin English would rather use regular speaking statements as:

  • I come naa, e lef

    (As soon as I came he left)

instead of:

  • Hardly had I arrived when he left

And also:

  • E no buy am alone, e san dress am (He not only bough it, but he dressed it too.)

Instead of:

  • Not only did he buy it, but also he dressed it.

Note: "San" is an Akan word meaning "again."
