Chapter 3 - Nouns

Nouns

Like in regular English, nouns are names. They:

  • Have number: can be singular or plural
  • Have gender: can be masculine, feminine, neither (neuter), or both (common)
  • Take determiners Indicate possession

Number

Number Formation of the plural doesn’t change a great deal in pidgin English. Many words that do not take an “s” in English, like sheep, do not in pidgin Either, even though there is license to add it. Most speakers will still say, for example,

  • We kill the sheep all
    (We have killed all the sheep)

But a few will say:

  • These sheeps be foolish
    (These sheep are foolish)

Gender

Gender

Just like in English, the concept of masculine and feminine is really limited as far as nouns go. “Woman” is feminine, and “man” is masculine, but this does not affect articles or verbs. Inanimate objects that can be referred to as “it” are neuter, and words like “person” and “baby” are said to have common gender because they can be either. In speaking, however nouns are used in pidgin mostly the same as in English.

Determiners

Determiners Nouns can be referred to by other words called determiners. Again, this is the same as in English. For example, in the sentence:

The men dey go

(The men are going)

The word “men” is the noun; the preceding “de”, meaning “the”, is a determiner. It points to the noun. Also:

Dem marshal the whole battalion.

(They have marshaled the whole battalion)

“Battalion” is the noun. “The whole” is the determiner.

The Indefinite Article

The Indefinite Article A note on the indefinite article “a” or “an”. It doesn’t exist in Pidgin English. It is simply omitted. For example:

My mommie buy football give me

(My mummy bought be a football)

I see aeroplane

(I saw an aeroplane)

Pidgin no get indefinite article

(Pidgin does not have an indefinite article)

A fun fact (totally by the way): In this regard Pidgin is similar to the language of the New Testament, koine Greek, which does not use an indefinite article either!

Possession

Possesion In Pidgin English nouns do not have possessive forms, strictly speaking. In regular English the possessive form adds an “ ’s ” to the end of a noun, or just an apostrophe to the end if the noun already ends in an “s”.

In pidgin you will hardly ever hear this construction. Instead, the basic construction uses the appropriate possessive pronoun, further corrupted into the personal objective form. We will explore this in the Chapter on Pronouns.

Noun Categories

Categories of Nouns The grammatical classifications of nouns remain the same:

Proper nouns are the given names of people and places (Eg. Jason, Mary, America, Labone, Ground Zero)

Common nouns refer to things (Eg. person, dog, frisbee, tourist) Count nouns are numerable (Eg. hat, pie, shirt, ticket) Non-count nouns are not (Eg. water, gari, light, food)

In Pidgin English, the distinction between count and non-count nouns is not as important as it is in regular English. Many non-count nouns like water will frequently be referred to in countable terms, such as:

Buy me one water

(in reference to bottles or sachets of water)

Have me two sugars

(in reference to cubes of sugar. "Have" means give.)

Plenty people

(instead of many people)

In addition, the word chao or chaw is used as an indefinite pronoun or determiner for both count and non-count nouns:

As a pronoun: I get chaw food

(I have a lot of food)

As a determiner: The farmer get chaw goats

(The farmer has many goats)

But generally the rule still holds: if it’s a count noun in regular English it is likely the same in Pidgin.

Abstract nouns denote intangible ideas and concepts (Eg. hope, fear, excitement, love) Concrete nouns are tangible (Eg. water, book, television, food) Collective nouns denote groups

In Pidgin English collective nouns appear to have been deprecated. You will hardly ever hear them used, and you shouldn’t either in most cases. Instead of collective nouns pidgin tends to use the plural of the noun with "chaw" for "many". Alternatively it will use the indefinite pronoun "some" in the indefinite and "the" (pronounced de) in the definite form. For example, instead of:

A herd of cows

or

The pride of lions

You would hear:

Some cows

The lions

In situations where the collective noun is the only word used, it may be retained in pidgin, but even this is rare. You could say:

Where the faculty dey?

(Where is the faculty?)

or

Where dey the faculty?

But often pidgin will use the individual elements of the group instead. So it’s more common to hear:

Where the lecturers dey?

(Where are the lecturers?)

And

Where the birds dey?

(Where are the birds)

instead of

Where the flock dey?
