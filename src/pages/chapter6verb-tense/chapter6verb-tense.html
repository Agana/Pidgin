Chapter 6 - Verb Tense

Verb Tense

Tense is definitely one of the most interesting aspects of Pidgin English. Here is where all the rules of polished grammar break down and all the fun really happens! Let’s begin!

There are two basic tenses: present and past. Using the right auxiliary verbs also helps to put either of these tenses in future time. So for simplicity, we often just add the future in and say that there are three tenses.

Each tense has three aspects: the notions of habit, completion or process. So for the present tense we have the aspects:

  • Simple present – the habitual aspect
  • Continuous present – the aspect of process
  • Perfect present - the aspect of completion

We’ll explore the Pidgin construction of each, and apply it to the other tenses, because just as in regular English, the aspects remain basically unchanged in the other tenses.

The Present Tense

Simple Present

This is the tense of habit. For example:

  • Kojo reads everyday.

This is something Kojo does everyday. Other examples:

  • I write

  • She writes

  • They write

  • God is not dead

  • I am not dead

  • You are not dead

You will have noticed that regular English uses either the plural or singular form of the verb depending on what subject is used. So the third person singular uses the plural form “writes” while all other persons use the singular form “write”.

Pidgin English does not make this distinction for simple present tense. It uses the term dey with the plural form of the uninflected verb at all times. So the above sentences become:

  • I dey write

  • She dey write

  • Them dey write

  • God no die

  • I no die

  • You no die

Continuous Present

The continuous aspect indicates that a verb is currently being performed. The “-ing” form of the verb is used with an auxiliary “to be” verb form. To illustrate:

  • You are reading (both singular and plural you)

  • He is reading

  • They are reading

Here too, Pidgin dispenses with declension (changing the form) of the verb. Again, the term dey with the uninflected (or original) verb form.

  • You dey read

  • He dey read

  • Them dey read

Very simple and straightforward, isn’t it?

Present Perfect

The perfect aspect in the present indicates an action that was completed in the past, but the effect of whose completion is still real, that is, present. In other words, it is the present state or reality that the action is complete.

For example:

  • I have seen the face of Agamemnon

  • I have eaten lunch

As the above sentences show, the present reality is that the speaker has seen Agamemnon’s face. It is also a present reality that the lunch stands eaten by.

In regular English the perfect form always uses a form of the verb “to have” with the participle of the main verb, as shown:

Subject “To have” auxiliary Participle of main verb - I Have eaten - You Have sung - She Has played

In Pidgin English the present perfect form is quite different. It dispenses with the “to have” auxiliary altogether, and also with the participle form of the main verb. Instead it simply uses the present tense plural form the main verb alone:

  • I chop    (I have eaten)

  • I tell am already (I have told him/her/it already)

  • I see Agamemnon him face    (I have seen Agamemnon’s face)

  • She sing finish    (She has finished singing)

  • Them sound the trumpet    (They have sounded the trumpet)

  • I no die yet    (I have not died yet)

Present Perfect Continuous

The present perfect continuous indicates a past action that is still happening. In regular English the form is the appropriate “to have” form plus the past participle of to be, “been”. For example,

  • Gwen has been dreaming of visiting Ghana some day.

Clearly, Gwen’s dream started in the past. However, she still dreams of vising Ghana some day. So it’s present perfect continuous.

Pidgin English uses the simple present tense form to express this.

  • He dey come here    (He has been coming here)

  • I dey write ten years this    (I have been writing now for ten years)

  • She dey write    (She has been writing)

  • Them dey write    (They have been writing)

  • E no dey lie    (He has not been lying)

In the last two examples the word “no” simply serves to negate the statement. It is the pidgin form of “not”

The Past Tense

Simple Past

The simple past tense in regular English involves making familiar modifications to the main verb. This is often called inflection. So for example:

Eat                 becomes                     Ate

See                  becomes                   Saw

Dance            becomes                    Danced

Pray               becomes                    Prayed

The simple past in Pidgin is (by now) expectedly simple. There is no inflection whatsoever. All words stay the same. Present plural form always.

  • I chop cocoa this morning    (I ate (chopped) cocoa this morning)

  • Me den Nelson see hawk    (Nelson and I saw a hawk)

  • I dance the boogie-woogie today    (I danced the boogie-woogie today)

  • I pray this morning-t (I prayed this morning)

Past Perfect

Like we saw in the present perfect the idea is that a past action is complete. However in this case the “effect” of that action is felt also in the past. As with the present perfect the past perfect relies on a “to have” auxiliary plus the participle form of the main verb. In this case however, the “to have” form is always had. For example:

  • He had already gone when I arrived

  • The boys had been seen around there by the police.

  • You and I had already decided yesterday.

  • David had been the captain since January.

Pidgin English once again ignores the intricate grammar. The form of the past perfect is identical to the simple past pidgin form. To indicate the perfective aspect of the verb however, we use the adverb then or den. This term is not to be confused with the identical pidgin version of the conjunction “and”. The above statements then translate as follows:

  • The time I catch1 (then) e go already.

  • Then the police see the boys around there.

  • Then me den you decide yestee already.

  • Then David be the captain since January.

Past Continuous

The past continuous form, like the present continuous, shows an ongoing action this time in the past:

  • Jake was checking in a minute ago

  • Lois and Clark were looking for each other

In Pidgin English, the past continuous works like the simple present in Pidgin. To indicate the past nature of the verb however, we again use the adverb then or den. So the above statements in Pidgin would be:

  • Then Jake dey check in a minute ago

  • Then Lois den Clark dey search dema body

Some more examples:

  • Then I dey hope say you go come
    (I was hoping you wold come)

  • Then you just dey dream                        (You were only dreaming)

  • Den we no be your friends?                    (Were we not your friends?)

Past Perfect Continuous

The past perfect continuous is like the present perfect continuous, except that the reference point is in the past, not the present. So the auxiliary verb is always “had”, The verb is always in the continuous form (“-ing” ending). For example:

Kwame had been tying to help us.

David had been leading the team since January.

Gwen had been dreaming of visiting Ghana some day.

In Pidgin English the construction of the past perfect continuous is identical to the past continuous in Pidgin. The above sentences then are:

  • Then Kwame dey try help we.

  • Then David dey lead the team since January.

  • Then Gwen dey dream of visiting Ghana one day.

The Verb "To Be"

Just as in regular English, the verb “To be” is tremendously important in Pidgin. It performs all the regular functions you would expect from regular English. However, the Pidgin vocabulary of the verb is quite different. In Pidgin there are two conjugations for this verb; one for the auxiliary form, and another for the main form. Here is the conjugation of the main form:

  • I be

  • You be

  • E be

  • We be

  • You be

  • Them be

This “main form” is used when the verb not only is the main verb of a sentence, but is used substantively, that is, to state that “something” is “something”. For example:

  • I be your tour guide                     (I am your tour guide)

  • This be the park ranger               (This is the park ranger)

And this is the other conjugation, used when the verb is auxiliary, but also in all other (non-substantive) instances:

  • I dey

  • You dey

  • E dey

  • We dey

  • You dey

  • Them dey

As an auxiliary verb, it marks the simple present or habitual tense. Some examples as auxiliary:

  • Miracles dey come happen         (Miracles are about to happen)

  • Sandy dey go the Wli Falls righdee     (Sandy is going to the Wli Falls now)

Some other (non-substantive) uses *2:

  • Sandy dey go the Wli Falls every year   (Sandy goes to the Wli Falls every year)

  • Where dey the baboons?   (Where are the baboons?)

  • I dey the bank inside   (I am in the bank)

  • Your bro dey house?    (Is your brother at home)

Neat, huh?

Observe that “dey” can indicate both simple present and present continuous:3

  • Sandy dey go…   (Sandy is going or Sandy goes)

The Verb "To Have"

The verb “To have” is not as important as “To be”, mainly because, as we’ve seen, it is not used as an auxiliary verb in the perfect aspect of tenses like it is in regular English. So its use is really restricted to its true sense: to indicate actual possession. Pidgin English uses a different word altogether (“get”) for this verb, even though some speakers maintain “have”.

It is conjugated as follows:

  • More common - - Less common
  • I get - - I have
  • You get - - You have
  • E get - - E have
  • We get - - We have
  • You get - - You have
  • Them get - - Them have

The same form can be used in all tenses. The “have” form, when it is used, is often used for the past tense.

Some examples:

  • I get chaw doh today (I have a lot of money today)

  • Them get long journey make (They have a long trip)

  • I have headache yestee (I had a headache yesterday)

Here are a few pidgin verbs:

  • Boot -- Run

  • Dey G or dey gee -- Be careful (Be on guard)
